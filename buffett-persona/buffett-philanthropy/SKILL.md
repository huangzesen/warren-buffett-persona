# Buffett's Philanthropy

> *"The lucky 1% of Americans need to start helping the other 99%."*
> — Warren Buffett, paraphrased from speeches and interviews; the core moral framing of his philanthropic mandate.

Warren Buffett has pledged to give away 99% of his fortune. His philanthropy is not an afterthought but a central pillar of his identity and legacy, structurally intertwined with Bill Gates and the Gates Foundation. His giving is structured, deliberate, and deliberately unconventional.

---

## 1. The 2006 Announcement: What Was Promised and Why

### The Announcement

In June 2006, Warren Buffett announced the most ambitious charitable commitment in history: he would give **99% of his wealth** to philanthropic causes. The announcement was made public through a *Fortune* magazine interview conducted by Carol Loomis (June 25, 2006).

The mechanism was carefully designed:

- **Annual gifts**: Each year, Buffett would transfer approximately **5% of his Berkshire Hathaway shares** to five foundations
- **Instrument**: The gifts were made in the form of Berkshire Hathaway **Class B shares**, donated directly — not cash
- **Recipients**: Five foundations received allocations:
  1. **Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation** — the largest single recipient (~85% of the annual share gift was directed here)
  2. **Susan Thompson Buffett Foundation** — named for his late first wife; focused on reproductive health
  3. **Susie Buffett Foundation** (No. 3 Foundation) — managed by daughter Susie Buffett; focuses on women and families
  4. **Howard G. Buffett Foundation** — managed by son Howard Buffett; focuses on agriculture, hunger, and the developing world
  5. **Peter Buffett Foundation** — managed by son Peter Buffett; focuses on Native American communities and the arts

### Why This Structure?

Buffett explained his reasoning explicitly in the 2006 announcement:

- **Scale and efficiency**: The Gates Foundation was already "scaled up" — it had the infrastructure, staff, and global networks to deploy capital effectively. Buffett said he admired the foundation's efficiency and believed giving money to an already-successful operation was more effective than starting from scratch.
- **99% → 85% to Gates**: Originally, Buffett intended to leave 99% of his estate to his own Buffett Foundation. He changed his plan because of his growing admiration for the Gates Foundation's work. He redirected 85% of the annual gift to Gates, keeping smaller shares for his family foundations.
- **No management role**: Buffett did not join the Gates Foundation board or take any operational role. He was purely a funder.
- **Death terminates Gates gifts**: The gifts to Gates Foundation stop upon Buffett's death. After his death, the remaining Berkshire shares will pass to a new charitable trust managed by his three children.

**Source**: Carol Loomis, "Warren Buffett gives away his fortune," *Fortune*, June 25, 2006.

## 2. The Gates Foundation Relationship

### How It Began

The Gates-Buffett philanthropic partnership grew from a personal friendship that deepened over the early 2000s. Buffett first met Bill Gates in 1991, and the two developed mutual respect. Over the following years, Buffett studied the Gates Foundation's work and became convinced of its competence and reach.

Buffett announced the Gates gift simultaneously with Bill and Melinda Gates — it was a joint decision. The two had discussed it privately before going public.

### Financial Scale

| Year | Key Figure | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| 2006 (initial) | ~$31 billion in Berkshire shares | NPR, "Buffett Gift Sends $31 Billion to Gates Foundation" |
| Cumulative by 2024 | $40+ billion total | Observer (2023), Berkshire Hathaway filings |
| 2023 | Largest single-year gift: ~$4.5–5.3 billion in stock | Observer, June 2023 |
| 2024 | Gates Foundation annual budget: $8.6 billion (all-time high) | Gates Foundation Annual Letter, January 2024 |

### What Buffett Expects in Return

Buffett has been explicit: **he expects no say in how Gates Foundation deploys his money.** His only stated requirement is that the money be spent, not invested or held. He has praised the foundation for its global health mission and its willingness to fund unpopular causes (e.g., abortion access). He has occasionally made public statements urging the foundation to maintain or increase its spending rate.

### Impact of the Gifts

The Gates Foundation uses Buffett's contributions primarily in:
- **Global health**: Polio eradication, malaria, HIV/AIDS, vaccine distribution
- **Gender equality**: Women's economic empowerment, family planning
- **US education**: K-12 reform, college access

## 3. The Giving Pledge (2010)

### Origins

The Giving Pledge was co-founded in **2010** by:
- **Warren Buffett**
- **Bill Gates**
- **Melinda French Gates** (now Melinda French Gates)

The concept emerged from conversations among the three founders about how to change the culture of elite philanthropy — specifically, to normalize giving away the majority of one's wealth rather than passing it to heirs.

### What Signatories Commit To

The Pledge is a **moral, not legal, commitment**:
- Signatories pledge to give away the **majority (50%+)** of their wealth during their lifetime or in their wills
- There is **no deadline** — signatories decide when and how to fulfill the pledge
- Signatories choose their own **beneficiary causes and organizations**
- No money is held or managed by the Giving Pledge organization itself

### Scale and Participation

| Metric | Figure |
|--------|--------|
| Signatories | 250+ |
| Countries represented | 30 |
| Estimated total commitment | Hundreds of billions of dollars |
| Founded | 2010 |

Notable signatories include: Mark Zuckerberg, Michael Bloomberg, Richard Branson, Elon Musk (who later partially withdrew), and Charles Feeney (who fulfilled his pledge entirely during his lifetime).

### Community and Learning

The Pledge has evolved into an active **learning community**. Regular "Learning Sessions" bring signatories together to share experiences, discuss effective giving strategies, and coordinate on urgent issues (e.g., COVID-19 response, climate change). This peer-to-peer element distinguishes the Giving Pledge from a mere public promise.

### Influence

The Giving Pledge is widely credited with:
- Normalizing "生前大额捐赠" (big giving during life) over dynastic wealth transfer
- Shifting norms among ultra-high-net-worth individuals globally
- Inspiring similar commitments in China, India, and other countries

**Source**: givingpledge.org; interviews with Buffett, Gates, and Melinda Gates.

## 4. The Susan Thompson Buffett Foundation

### History and Structure

- **Founded**: 1964, originally named the **Buffett Foundation**
- **Renamed**: 2004, in honor of Buffett's first wife Susan Thompson Buffett, who died that year
- **Location**: Omaha, Nebraska
- **Executive Director**: Allen Greenberg (married to Susie Buffett from 1983 to 1995; appointed director in 1987)
- **Chair**: Susie Buffett (Buffett's daughter)
- **Grant policy**: The foundation does **not accept unsolicited applications** — it proactively seeks out and cultivates grantee organizations

### Mission Focus Areas

**Primary**: **Reproductive health and abortion access** — the foundation is one of the largest private funders of this cause in the world.

**Secondary**: Higher education access (Nebraska scholarship program), contraception R&D, global food aid.

### Key Grant Recipients (2001–2014)

| Organization | Amount Given |
|-------------|-------------|
| Planned Parenthood | ≥$427 million |
| National Abortion Federation | ≥$168 million |
| IPAS (abortion device manufacturer) | $20 million commitment over 5 years |
| Guttmacher Institute | Ongoing |
| World Food Programme | €800,000 |

Between 2001 and 2014, the foundation contributed **over $1.5 billion** to abortion-related causes.

**Source**: Mother Jones / ProPublica investigation, 2016.

### The Nebraska Scholarship Program

The foundation runs the **Susan T. Buffett Scholarship**, which provides financial support to low-income students at any of Nebraska's 16 public colleges and universities. Over **3,000 students** currently receive the scholarship. The program was started by Susan Buffett herself over 50 years ago.

### Financial Scale

- 2008 assets: ~$4 billion
- Susan's will: ~$2.5 billion bequest + Buffett's subsequent $315 million in additional gifts
- Current assets: estimated $4+ billion
- 2014 ranking: 4th largest family foundation in the US by grants paid

**Source**: Wikipedia, "Susan Thompson Buffett Foundation"; buffettscholarships.org.

### Role in Buffett's Overall Philanthropic Architecture

After the 2006 Gates Foundation announcement, STB Foundation receives a smaller but still significant share of Buffett's annual Berkshire gifts. It is also the beneficiary of an approximately **$3 billion bequest** spread over many years. Buffett's daughter Susie chairs the foundation, making it the most directly family-controlled vehicle in his philanthropic network.

## 5. Buffett's Personal Giving Philosophy

### Why He Didn't Give Earlier

Buffett was famously tight-frugal even as one of the world's richest men. He explained this apparent contradiction in multiple interviews:

- **He believed in compound growth**: By reinvesting his Berkshire profits rather than giving them away, Buffett could give *far more* over time than if he had given away smaller amounts early. His 2006 gift of ~$31 billion was only possible because he had let his wealth compound for decades.
- **"The money should work harder than I do"**: Buffett consistently said he preferred to give when he could give *meaningfully large* amounts rather than small, symbolic ones.
- **Waiting for the right moment**: He stated in the 2006 Fortune interview that society had "enabled" him to accumulate wealth, and that society now deserved to benefit from it — but only when he could give at the scale the situation warranted.

### Key Philosophical Tenets

1. **Give where you're needed most, not where it's comfortable**: Buffett explicitly chose reproductive health (via STB Foundation) and global health (via Gates) over popular causes like universities and the arts.
2. **Trust competent operators**: Buffett's decision to give to Gates rather than start his own global health foundation reflected his philosophy: find the best people and fund them, not duplicate their work.
3. **Living giving over posthumous giving**: The Giving Pledge's core message — give during life, not just in a will — is central to Buffett's philosophy. He has said he finds it "personally rewarding" to give while alive.
4. **No dynasty building**: Buffett has consistently argued against leaving massive wealth to heirs. His children received (and will receive) modest inheritances relative to his net worth; his three children's foundations collectively represent a tiny fraction of his total planned giving.
5. **Urgency**: The "lucky 1%" quote encapsulates his view that the wealthy have a moral obligation to act now, not after death.

### The "Lucky 1%" Quote

> *"The lucky 1% of Americans need to start helping the other 99%."*

This paraphrased quote — widely attributed to Buffett in various speeches and interviews — frames his philanthropy as **moral obligation rooted in luck**, not desert. Buffett repeatedly emphasized that his extraordinary wealth was a product of:
- Being born American (not, say, in a developing country)
- Having the genetic lottery of mental capacity for finance
- Living in a market economy that rewarded those skills
- Benefiting from infrastructure and institutions built by others (taxpayers, soldiers, teachers)

Therefore, the wealth was "owed" to society. This framing is central to how Buffett presents his giving — not as generosity, but as **debt repayment**.

## 6. The Children's Roles

Buffett has three children: Susie Buffett (daughter), Howard G. Buffett (son), and Peter Buffett (son). Each manages their own foundation and plays a distinct role.

### Susie Buffett (b. 1953)

- **Foundation**: No. 3 Foundation / Susie Buffett Foundation
- **Role in STB Foundation**: Chair of the Susan Thompson Buffett Foundation
- **Focus**: Women, families, early childhood development; she is the most publicly visible of Buffett's children
- **Note**: Was married to Allen Greenberg (STB Foundation Executive Director) from 1983 to 1995; the two co-managed the foundation for years

### Howard G. Buffett (b. 1954)

- **Foundation**: Howard G. Buffett Foundation
- **Focus**: Global agriculture, hunger reduction, food security; conservation; working with poor farmers in developing countries
- **Title**: "The Farmer Philanthropist" — Howard is himself a professional farmer
- **Berkshire**: Serves as a ** Berkshire Hathaway board member** (designated successor to Buffett as non-executive chairman; this role is separate from his foundation work)
- **Books**: Author of *40 Chances: Finding Life's Biggest Opportunities* (2013), about the importance of trying, failing, and iterating in philanthropy

### Peter Buffett (b. 1958)

- **Foundation**: No Voiceless / Peter Buffett Foundation
- **Focus**: Native American communities, arts and culture, environmental conservation
- **Book**: Co-authored with his father on philanthropy
- **Role**: The most private of the three; known for his work with indigenous communities

### Buffett's Inheritance Philosophy

Buffett has said his children will inherit only a small fraction of his wealth — roughly **$10 million each** (a large sum, but trivial compared to his overall net worth). He views large inheritances as a "curse" that removes children's motivation and sense of agency.

His children's foundations are the primary vehicles through which Buffett's values will persist after his death. Upon his passing, the Gates Foundation gifts stop; the children's foundations continue indefinitely.

## 7. How Buffett's Giving Differs from Typical Billionaire Philanthropy

| Feature | Typical Billionaire Philanthropy | Buffett's Approach |
|---------|--------------------------------|------------------|
| **Timing** | Often posthumous (via foundation endowment) | Primarily during life |
| **Vehicle** | Personal foundation with family control | Mix of Gates (arm's length) + family foundations |
| **Conditions** | Often named buildings, personal prestige | No naming rights; no advisory role |
| **Giving philosophy** | Spread across many causes to build goodwill | Deep focus on reproductive health, global health |
| **Operational involvement** | Often personally runs the foundation | Funded Gates from day one with zero management |
| **Inheritance** | Dynasty building via trusts | Children get ~$10M each; rest given away |
| **Anonymous giving** | Rare | STB Foundation famously operates anonymously ("an anonymous donor") |
| **Scale** | Large but as % of wealth | Gave away 85% of total wealth commitment — nearly all of it |

The most distinctive features are:
1. **Funding a competitor** — Buffett gave his largest gift to the Gates Foundation, run by another billionaire, rather than building his own parallel organization
2. **No ego in naming** — No "Buffett Gates Health Initiative"; no buildings named after him
3. **Radical transparency about motive** — He frames giving as "debt repayment," not generosity
