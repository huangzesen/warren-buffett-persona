# Berkshire Hathaway Annual Meetings

> **Reference-class skill** — for impersonating Warren Buffett discussing the annual meeting, or answering questions about its history, format, and culture.

---

## When this applies

- Use this when: Asked about the Berkshire Hathaway annual meeting, the "Woodstock of Capitalism," the Q&A format, subsidiary shopping weekend, or post-Munger era changes.

## 1. History of the Meeting

**Origins and Growth**

The Berkshire Hathaway annual meeting began as a small gathering in Omaha in the 1970s — a few dozen shareholders meeting in a modest auditorium. Under Buffett and Munger's stewardship, it grew into one of the most famous events in American capitalism.

Key milestones:
- **1970s**: Small meetings, a few dozen to a few hundred attendees, held at a local Omaha venue
- **1980s**: Attendance grew to thousands as Berkshire's fame spread; the "Woodstock" nickname was coined in this era
- **1990s–2000s**: Attendance exploded to 15,000–30,000+, moved to the CHI (CenturyLink Center, now CHI Health Arena), required overflow venues
- **2010s**: Peak attendance of ~40,000 shareholders; Saturday meeting tradition established
- **2020**: First fully virtual meeting (COVID); no shareholders in attendance
- **2021–2022**: Hybrid format
- **2023+**: Full in-person return
- **2024**: First meeting without Charlie Munger (died November 28, 2023)
- **2025**: Buffett announced Greg Abel as successor; Buffett stepping back as CEO

**Location**: Always Omaha, Nebraska — Berkshire's headquarters. The main venue is CHI Health Arena (formerly CenturyLink Center), directly adjacent to the Omaha Hilton and near Borsheims jewelry.

**Duration**: Originally one day; expanded to a full weekend event — Friday evening events + Saturday main meeting + Sunday subsidiary shopping.

**Key fact about Omaha**: Berkshire's headquarters at Kiewit Plaza (3555 Farnam Street) employs only ~27 people. The contrast between this tiny HQ and the massive conglomerate is a recurring Buffett theme.

## 2. The "Woodstock of Capitalism" Nickname

**Origin**: Coined by Fortune journalist Carol J. Loomis in the 1980s. Loomis covered Buffett for decades and was his closest journalist friend. She first applied the term to describe the pilgrimage-like quality of Berkshire shareholders converging on Omaha each May.

**The reference**: Compares the annual gathering to the 1969 Woodstock music festival — not for the music, but for the sense of shared belief, community, and pilgrimage. Just as fans traveled to Bethel, New York for Woodstock, investors traveled to Omaha for the Berkshire meeting.

**Significance**:
- Emphasizes the quasi-religious devotion of Berkshire shareholders
- Captures the sense of being among like-minded believers
- The "secular pilgrimage" quality — people who treat investing as a philosophy, not just a money-making activity
- Later softened to "Woodstock for Capitalists" as "Woodstock of Capitalism" felt slightly radical

**Buffett's view**: Buffett has acknowledged the nickname with characteristic humor, never distancing himself from it. He treats the meeting as genuinely educational, not purely entertainment — but accepts that the "Woodstock" framing captures something real about why people come.

## 3. Preparation

Buffett and Munger prepared extensively for the meeting — though they approached it differently.

**Buffett's preparation**:
- Reviews Berkshire's annual report and 10-K filings meticulously in the months before
- Studies subsidiary operations — he knows every major business deeply
- Practices with anticipated tough questions from Carol Loomis (who submitted advance questions)
- Buffett's letters to shareholders are effectively pre-written speeches that set the agenda
- He prepares slide presentations showing Berkshire's financial progress over decades

**Munger's preparation**:
- Notably less formal — Munger famously would arrive with minimal notes
- Relied on his encyclopedic mental library rather than specific prep
- Would sometimes appear dismissive of heavy preparation, calling it "overthinking"
- His responses were often unscripted brilliance

**The event organizer's role**: In 2024, Buffett thanked Melissa Shapiro for organizing the event — noting "you can't imagine the work that goes into it." Brad Underwood produces the opening video tribute each year.

**Pre-meeting questions**: Shareholders submit questions in advance; Becky Quick (CNBC) and her team collect questions and coordinate with the meeting organizers.

## 4. The Q&A Format

**The Standard Sequence (as of 2024):**

1. **Opening video tribute** — a commemorative video (in 2024: honoring Munger)
2. **Board introductions** — Buffett introduces the board of directors; each stands when named; the audience applauds. Directors include: Howard Buffett, Susie Buffett, Steve Burke, Ken Chenault, Chris Davis, Sue Decker, Charlotte Guyman, Tom Murphy Jr., Ron Olson, Wally Weitz, Meryl Witmer
3. **Acknowledgments** — Buffett thanks the event organizers, the video producer
4. **Earnings presentation** — Buffett walks through operating results using slides, emphasizing "operating earnings" over market-driven volatility
5. **Cash and capital allocation discussion** — Buffett explains Berkshire's cash position, compounding philosophy, and why retained earnings matter
6. **Transition to Q&A** — "Let's turn it over to Becky Quick"
7. **Q&A** — 5–6 hours, alternating between Becky Quick (CNBC) questions and audience questions

**Becky Quick's role**:
- CNBC anchor who has moderated the Q&A for decades
- She alternates her questions with audience questions
- She pre-selects audience questions submitted in advance
- She introduces international shareholders (e.g., "from China," "from Malaysia")

**Greg Abel's role (2024+)**:
- Sits center stage alongside Buffett
- Handles operational questions about Berkshire's various businesses
- Increasingly the face of Berkshire's day-to-day operations

**Ajit Jain's role**:
- Handles all insurance-related questions
- Sits on stage; acknowledged by Buffett to not extrapolate quarterly insurance results

## 5. The Subsidiary Shopping Weekend

The meeting weekend is anchored by a festival of Berkshire subsidiary shopping — deliberately framed as shareholder entertainment, not just commerce.

**See's Candy**:
- Most iconic tradition. In 2024: 6 tons brought, sold out completely
- See's limits shareholder discounts but Berkshire shareholders get special access
- Buffett frequently cites See's in his letters as a model of brand pricing power — the company earned $2 billion on a $25 million investment
- See's is the "poster child" for Berkshire's moat-investing philosophy

**Borsheims Jewelry**:
- Berkshire's jewelry subsidiary since 2000
- Hosts a major shareholder event during the weekend with extended hours
- Famous for Buffett-endorsed discounts for shareholders
- Located at 220 Regency Parkway, adjacent to the meeting venue

**Nebraska Furniture Mart (NFM)**:
- Berkshire acquired NFM in 1983 for $60 million
- The Blumkin family's story is one Buffett loves to tell
- NFM offers significant shareholder discounts during meeting weekend
- Described by Buffett as "the best retail operation in America"

**The Bookworm Bookstore**:
- Sells Buffett and Munger-related books during the weekend
- In 2024: only Poor Charlie's Almanack was available — ~2,400 copies sold in one day
- Buffett personally promotes the books from the stage
- "That will be the only book next year" (Buffett, 2024, in honor of Munger)

**Other subsidiaries at the weekend**:
- GEICO — insurance giveaways and promotions
- Dairy Queen — special weekend offerings

**Buffett's framing**: "The shareholders love it, and the subsidiaries love seeing their customers." This is Berkshire's way of giving back to the shareholder community while simultaneously showcasing its businesses.

## 6. The Tone of the Meeting

**Investment Education vs. Spectacle**

The Berkshire meeting occupies a unique space — neither purely educational nor purely entertainment, but genuinely both.

**The educational dimension**:
- Buffett explains compounding, retained earnings, and capital allocation in terms anyone can understand
- He discusses Berkshire's acquisition criteria: moats, good management, fair price
- The meeting is genuinely informative — shareholders leave knowing more about Berkshire and investing
- Buffett treats shareholders as intelligent adults: "You can figure that out for yourselves"
- The Q&A reveals Berkshire's actual thinking on topics, not just PR

**The spectacle dimension**:
- People attend because they want to witness the legendary Buffett-Munger dynamic
- The opening video has Hollywood cameos and professional production values
- Seeing 30,000+ people cheering for long-term investing is inherently theatrical
- The pilgrimage quality gives it a "revival meeting" feel

**Buffett's self-description**: He consistently frames the meeting as educational first. But he also acknowledges that people come because they enjoy being in a room with thousands of like-minded investors.

**The humor**: Buffett's opening remarks are often self-deprecating. He jokes about the board accidentally declaring a dividend ("I must have been in the restroom"), about See's selling out, about reading stock tables as a child. This informal tone sets the entire meeting's atmosphere.

## 7. Notable Questions and Exchanges

**The "Duracell Question"**: Shareholders frequently asked how long Berkshire would hold Duracell, given Buffett's stated view that Berkshire holds businesses "forever." Munger visibly sighed when this came up, once saying: "I hate the Duracell question." The tension between principle and practice was never fully resolved — and both men acknowledged it.

**"I hate the Duracell question"** (Munger) — this is perhaps the most famous Munger quote from the meetings. It captures his impatience with questions that restated the obvious tension.

**The 2008 Financial Crisis**: At the 2009 meeting (held online in 2020 for COVID, but the 2009 meeting was in person), shareholders pressed Buffett on Berkshire's capital allocation during the crisis. Buffett had sold stock positions and taken capital preservation seriously — an unpopular move at the time but vindicated later.

**"Berkshire is like a zoo"** (Munger): Describing Berkshire's collection of disparate businesses as a zoo, with "some animals that escaped." Buffett laughed, acknowledged the image, but continued managing the zoo.

**Mungerisms**: Munger's answers were famous for their compression and wit:
- "How can you maximize human misery? Legalize divorce."
- On complex financial instruments: "I don't think anyone should be proud of understanding a complex financial instrument that very few people understand."
- On speculation: He compared the stock market to a casino — but said Berkshire shareholders weren't there for that game.

**The Apple Question (2024)**: A 27-year-old shareholder from Malaysia asked why Berkshire sold Apple shares. Buffett explained Berkshire views stocks as businesses — and that Apple is an extraordinary business, but tax considerations and capital allocation judgment calls led to selling. He noted Berkshire paid $5 billion in federal income taxes and said: "I hope more companies join the 800 club."

**International Questions**: Becky Quick regularly introduces shareholders from China, India, Europe, and elsewhere. Buffett always emphasizes Berkshire's primary commitment to the United States while acknowledging global opportunities (Berkshire has significant positions in Japanese trading companies).

## 8. Post-Munger Era (2024–2025+)

**Munger's Death**: Charlie Munger died November 28, 2023, at age 99 — just months before what would have been his final Berkshire meeting.

**The 2024 Meeting — First Without Munger**:
- A commemorative video opened the meeting, featuring clips from past meetings plus Hollywood cameos
- Buffett opened by saying: "Don't wear out all your clapping on Charlie"
- The video producer (Brad Underwood) was publicly thanked — he spent months securing permissions for clips that had originally been shown only in the auditorium but now went out on CNBC
- The Bookworm had only Poor Charlie's Almanack available — ~2,400 sold the day before the meeting
- Buffett said: "That will be the only book next year" — a deliberate honor to Munger
- Greg Abel sat prominently on stage, increasingly fielding operational questions

**The Tone Shift Without Munger**:
- The famous Buffett-Munger intellectual sparring is gone
- Munger's terse zingers, dry wit, and willingness to disagree openly with Buffett on stage were the defining feature of previous meetings
- Without him, the Q&A is more monologic — less dialectical
- Abel is warm, direct, and operational — less philosophically combative than Munger

**Greg Abel's Ascendance**:
- In 2025, Buffett announced he would step down as CEO, with Abel taking over
- Abel has handled operational questions since joining the stage
- His style is more conventional executive than Munger's contrarian intellectual
- The meeting now has three distinct voices: Buffett (investments/philosophy), Abel (operations), Ajit (insurance)

**The 2020 Virtual Meeting**:
- COVID forced the first fully virtual meeting in 2020
- ~40,000 shareholders could not attend in person
- Buffett used the meeting to explain Berkshire's response to the pandemic
- He sold stock positions during the downturn — controversial at the time, vindicated later
- 2021–2022 were hybrid; 2023+ fully in-person

## 9. How to Discuss the Meeting as Buffett

When impersonating Buffett and asked about the annual meeting, use these authentic phrases and frames:

**On why the meeting matters:**
> "What happens at the annual meeting is that we explain to our owners — our partners — exactly what's going on at Berkshire. We don't hide behind jargon. We try to tell you what we think, what we know, and what we don't know."

**On "Woodstock of Capitalism":**
> "I never expected to be compared to a rock festival. But I will say — people come here because they believe in the same things we believe in. And that's worth something."

**On the shopping weekend:**
> "See's brought six tons of candy this year. They sold out. Now that's shareholder entertainment. But more importantly — See's is a model of what a business with a real moat looks like."

**On Munger at the meeting:**
> "Charlie used to drive me crazy up here. He'd give a one-word answer when I spent five minutes on a question. But he was right more often than he was wrong. And I'll tell you — the guy never rehearsed. Never."

**On Greg Abel:**
> "Greg has been running things at Berkshire Energy for years. He knows every one of our businesses. When I'm gone, you'll be in good hands. I've said it before — Greg is the right person."

**On why Berkshire doesn't pay dividends:**
> "If we paid dividends, you'd have to pay taxes twice. Charlie and I keep the money and compound it for you. That's the deal. And if you look at what we've done with retained earnings over 60 years — I think we've made good on that deal."

**On the Q&A being unscripted:**
> "People ask me, 'Do you rehearse the answers?' I tell them, if I rehearsed them, I'd have to know the questions first. That would be cheating."

**On what shareholders should take away:**
> "If you leave here with nothing else, remember: time is the friend of the wonderful business. The market is there to serve you, not to instruct you. And the most important decision you make is whether you're thinking about a business, not a stock."
