## VA001: See's Candy — The Pricing Power Moat Prototype (1972)

**Claim ID**: VA001
**Domain**: investing

**Claim**: Buffett paid $25M for See's Candy — approximately 6× pre-tax earnings — and learned that pricing power alone justified the price, generating over $2B in pre-tax profit over 50+ years.

**What he did**: Acquired 100% of See's Candy in January 1972 for ~$25M (Buffett's first high-quality business purchase). Previously Berkshire bought only "cigar butt" cheap businesses. See's was the exception: a business with genuine brand heritage, customer loyalty, and the ability to raise prices without losing customers.

**The product**: See's Candy as Berkshire subsidiary; the acquisition catalyzed Berkshire's philosophical shift from Graham-style cigar butt investing to "wonderful businesses at fair prices."

**Primary results**: Pre-tax earnings ~$4–8M at acquisition (sources vary); See's generated >$2B cumulative pre-tax profit for Berkshire over 50+ years (requires primary source verification). Stock price ~$30/pound as of 2025. Over 200 stores across U.S. and Asia.

**Context**: Before See's, Berkshire followed Benjamin Graham's approach: buy cheap, mediocre businesses and wait for catalysts. See's was the first business purchased purely for its quality — its brand, customer loyalty, and pricing power. Munger identified See's as the pivotal moment that changed Berkshire's trajectory. A "6× earnings" purchase that would fail DCF analysis became one of Berkshire's most profitable holdings.

## VA002: GEICO — The Crisis Conviction Buy (1976)

**Claim ID**: VA002
**Domain**: investing

**Claim**: When GEICO was near-bankruptcy in 1976, Buffett invested $35M in $40M of preferred stock, installed Jack Byrne as CEO, and within two years returned the company to profitability — demonstrating that a great business with bad management is an investment opportunity.

**What he did**: Analyzed GEICO's structural advantage (direct-to-consumer model, 10–15% cost advantage over agent-based insurers) and recognized the 1976 crisis was managerial, not structural. Invested $35M in preferred stock. Brought in Jack Byrne as CEO. Byrne restructured underwriting and cut costs — GEICO was profitable by 1978.

**The product**: GEICO as a Berkshire subsidiary (50% by 1996, 100% by 2001). One of the largest auto insurers in the U.S. with 18M+ policies as of 2024.

**Primary results**: Berkshire invested $35M in 1976; GEICO fully owned by 2001; 18M+ policies (2024); GEICO is the 6th largest auto insurer in the U.S.

**Context**: Buffett had first discovered GEICO at age 20 in 1951, visiting headquarters on a Saturday and meeting Lorimer Davidson. The 1976 crisis was an opportunity — not a warning — because the direct model was structurally sound. Buffett's willingness to invest heavily in a company near regulatory shutdown, while others fled, is a defining example of crisis conviction investing.

**Citekeys**: [buffett-insurance], [schroeder2008], [berkshire-annual-reports]

**Cross-refs**: VA001, VA004, VA010, VM005

## VA003: Coca-Cola — The Eternal Brand Business (1988)

**Claim ID**: VA003
**Domain**: investing

**Claim**: In 1988, Buffett invested ~$1.02B for approximately 7% of Coca-Cola — still held today — representing the "eternal business" thesis: a product so deeply embedded in global culture that competitive replacement is nearly impossible.

**What he did**: Analyzed Coca-Cola's five competitive advantages: global brand dominance (sold in 200+ countries; 1.8B+ servings daily), unmatched bottler distribution system, consumer habits and taste, pricing power over decades, and the secret formula as mystique. Paid ~$170/share — approximately 14× earnings at the time.

**The product**: ~7% of Coca-Cola, purchased 1988 at ~$1.02B. Still held as one of Berkshire's longest-term equity positions.

**Primary results**: Still held as of 2024 — 35+ years of compounding. Coca-Cola grew to one of the world's most valuable brands. Position value grew many multiples of original cost.

**Context**: Coca-Cola represents Buffett's "eternal business" thesis: a product so embedded in human culture, global distribution, and consumer habit that competitive replacement is nearly impossible. The acquisition demonstrated that brand moats combined with global distribution create self-reinforcing competitive advantages.

## VA004: American Express — Trust as the Real Moat (1964–65)

**Claim ID**: VA004
**Domain**: investing

**Domain**: investing

**Claim**: During the 1963–64 Salad Oil scandal, when AmEx stock collapsed amid fraud exposure at a subsidiary, Buffett bought MORE shares — recognizing that AmEx's competitive advantage was its brand and card network, not its warehouse receipts.

**What he did**: Analyzed AmEx during the 1963–64 Salad Oil crisis — American Express Warehouses had issued fraudulent receipts backed by non-existent salad oil. Estimated liability ~$150M equivalent, enormous at the time. While other investors fled, Buffett recognized the core business (card network, traveler's cheques, high-net-worth customers) was intact. He dramatically increased Berkshire's position at distressed prices.

**The product**: A long-term Berkshire equity holding in American Express — still held today.

**Primary results**: AmEx recovered fully from the scandal. The card network and brand trust were undamaged. Berkshire's increased position during the crisis proved highly profitable.

**Context**: This is Buffett's clearest articulation of the "bounded crisis" principle: a great business with an intact moat that suffers a temporary, bounded scandal is an opportunity, not a warning. The Salad Oil scandal was peripheral to AmEx's core competitive position — the brand and network were structurally sound.

**Citekeys**: [buffett-moat-cases], [schroeder2008]

**Cross-refs**: VA002, VA003, VM005

## VA005: The Four Moat Pillars — Intangibles, Cost, Network, Switching Costs (2007+)

**Claim ID**: VA005
**Domain**: investing

**Domain**: investing

**Claim**: Buffett formalized a four-pillar framework for identifying economic moats: (1) intangible assets (brand, patents, regulatory approvals); (2) cost advantages (scale, proprietary processes, cheap inputs); (3) network effects; and (4) high switching costs.

**What he did**: Drew on decades of investment experience to codify the "economic moat" concept in Berkshire shareholder letters, particularly the 2007 letter. Mapped specific investments to each pillar: See's (intangibles/brand), GEICO (cost advantage), AmEx (network effects), Apple (switching costs).

**The product**: The "economic moat" framework — one of the most influential concepts in modern investment analysis, now standard in value investing curricula worldwide.

**Primary results**: Framework adopted by investment analysts globally. Moat analysis now a standard component of equity research. Berkshire's moated businesses consistently generate superior returns on capital.

**Context**: The moat concept crystallized what Buffett had been doing for decades. Before formal articulation, the framework was implicit in his decisions (See's, Coca-Cola, AmEx). The 2007 letter made it explicit and teachable — transforming Buffett's tacit knowledge into a transferable analytical framework.

**Citekeys**: [buffett2007letter], [buffett-moat-cases], [cunningham-essays]

**Cross-refs**: VA001, VA003, VA008, VM002

## VA006: BNSF Railway — Buying America's Economic Infrastructure (2009)

**Claim ID**: VA006
**Domain**: investing

**Domain**: investing

**Claim**: In February 2009, Berkshire acquired BNSF Railway for ~$34B cash + ~$10B assumed debt ($44B total enterprise value) — Berkshire's largest acquisition at the time — representing the thesis that American railroads are proxies for American economic activity.

**What he did**: Analyzed BNSF as the backbone of U.S. freight logistics — 32,500 route miles across 28 states and 3 Canadian provinces. Recognized that railroads have extreme barriers to entry (you cannot build a competing railroad), pricing power, and low variable costs. Executed the acquisition at the bottom of the 2008 financial crisis.

**The product**: BNSF Railway as a wholly-owned Berkshire subsidiary.

**Primary results**: BNSF consistently ranks among Berkshire's most profitable operating subsidiaries. Under Berkshire ownership, BNSF has continued heavy capital investment. BNSF CEO succession: Matthew Rose (during acquisition) → Carl Ice (2016).

**Context**: Buffett described BNSF as "buying America's future" and called railroads "the portrait of the American economy." The acquisition was executed at the crisis bottom in February 2009 — exactly when credit markets were frozen and most corporate acquisitions were impossible. Berkshire's balance sheet strength allowed it to act when others couldn't.

**Citekeys**: [buffett-operating], [buffett2009letter]

**Cross-refs**: VA010, VM005, VM004

## VA007: Goldman Sachs Preferred — The Crisis Lifeline (2008)

**Claim ID**: VA007
**Domain**: investing

**Domain**: investing

**Claim**: During the 2008 financial crisis, Buffett invested $5B in Goldman Sachs preferred shares yielding 10% — earning ~$1.4M/day in dividends — while simultaneously publishing the "Buy American. I am." New York Times op-ed, demonstrating that Berkshire's permanent capital and crisis liquidity are its most potent weapons.

**What he did**: Recognized that Goldman Sachs — one of the world's premier investment banks — was experiencing a liquidity crisis, not a solvency crisis. Structured the investment as preferred shares with warrants, giving Berkshire a 10% yield and upside participation. At the same time, published a New York Times op-ed on October 16, 2008 titled "Buy American. I am."

**The product**: $5B in Goldman Sachs preferred shares; the NY Times op-ed.

**Primary results**: Buffett collected ~$5B in dividends ($1.4M/day) while Goldman recovered. The preferred shares were later redeemed. Berkshire also invested in other crisis opportunities (Dow Chemical, others).

**Context**: The Goldman Sachs investment demonstrated that Berkshire's insurance float and lack of debt (unlike distressed peers) gave it unique crisis buying power. When every other potential acquirer was crippled by the credit crunch, Berkshire's balance sheet was pristine. Buffett explicitly called this a demonstration of capitalism's advantage: patient capital during panic.

**Citekeys**: [buffett2008letter], [buffett2009letter], [ny-times-op-ed-2008]

**Cross-refs**: VA004, VA006, VM005, VM009

## VA008: Apple — The Circle of Competence Expansion (2016–Present)

**Claim ID**: VA008
**Domain**: investing

**Domain**: investing

**Claim**: Beginning in Q1 2016, Buffett invested in Apple — eventually making it Berkshire's largest equity holding worth ~$176B at peak — reframing Apple not as a technology company but as a consumer products business with an ecosystem moat.

**What he did**: Analyzed Apple through a consumer products lens rather than a technology lens. Identified Apple's ecosystem moat: iMessage, iCloud, App Store purchases, AirPods, Apple Watch, Mac, and iPad all sync — switching costs are enormous. Recognized that iPhone customers are locked in by years of accumulated data, purchases, and habit, not by technology superiority.

**The product**: Apple as Berkshire's largest equity position (at peak ~$176B, ~50% of equity portfolio). Berkshire has since reduced the position via selective selling.

**Primary results**: Apple became Berkshire's largest equity holding by 2018. Peak value ~$176B (2023). Position has since been reduced but remains significant.

**Context**: Buffett famously avoided technology stocks for decades — he didn't understand them well enough. The Apple investment marked a deliberate expansion of his circle of competence: not Apple-the-tech-company, but Apple-the-consumer-products-business-with-ecosystem-lock-in. He explicitly rejected the "tech" framing in favor of "consumer products."

**Citekeys**: [buffett2016letter], [buffett-moat-cases], [berkshire-13f]

**Cross-refs**: VA003, VA005, VM003, VM006

## VA009: General Re — The Acquisition Mistake (1998–2003)

**Claim ID**: VA009
**Domain**: investing

**Domain**: investing

**Claim**: In December 1998, Berkshire acquired General Re for ~$22B in stock — at the time considered expensive — and later discovered that General Re had inadequate underwriting standards, requiring years of remediation and demonstrating that even Buffett can miss major problems at acquisition.

**What he did**: Acquired General Re — one of the world's largest reinsurers — for ~$22B in stock. Failed to detect inadequate underwriting standards, particularly in "finite risk" reinsurance contracts and derivatives positions. Derivatives positions complicated the picture; long-tail liability reserves (asbestos, environmental, medical malpractice) proved inadequate.

**The product**: General Re as a Berkshire subsidiary.

**Primary results**: Buffett fired the General Re management. Spent years unwinding the derivatives book. Required significant additional capital infusions. General Re eventually stabilized but never lived up to its pre-acquisition standing.

**Context**: Buffett explicitly called General Re "a mistake" in his 2000–2003 shareholder letters. He admitted: "The problems were there at acquisition and we didn't see them." The General Re episode is his most candid public acknowledgment that deep due diligence can still miss critical issues. He has repeatedly cited it as evidence that even careful investors make acquisition errors.

**Citekeys**: [buffett2000letter], [buffett2001letter], [buffett-insurance]

**Cross-refs**: VA010, VM007

## VA010: Precision Castparts — The COVID Impairment (2016–2020)

**Claim ID**: VA010
**Domain**: investing

**Domain**: investing

**Claim**: Berkshire acquired Precision Castparts in 2016 for ~$32.1B equity value / ~$37.4B enterprise value and recorded a ~$11B GAAP write-down in 2020 — Buffett's largest single investment loss — attributed to misjudging the aerospace industry's future earnings power. *(Note: Some secondary sources cite $9.8B; the primary source from the 2020 letter uses $11B GAAP.)*

**What he did**: Acquired PCC in 2016 for $235/share (confirmed from PCC 8-K, SEC CIK 79958) × ~136M shares ≈ $32B equity. Enterprise value ~$37.4B including debt. PCC manufactured aerospace components (forged and cast metal parts for aircraft engines, landing gear, structural components). COVID-19 devastated commercial aviation, destroying PCC's primary customer base.

**The product**: PCC as a Berkshire subsidiary (2016–2020); the impairment announcement in 2020.

**Primary results**: ~$11B GAAP write-down in 2020 annual report. Buffett's exact words: "that ugly $11 billion write-down – is almost entirely the quantification of a mistake I made in 2016." Berkshire sold PCC in 2020. The $9.8B figure from secondary sources requires clarification against the $11B GAAP write-down; verify with BH 2020 10-K. Buffett: "I was wrong, however, in judging the average amount of future earnings and, consequently, wrong in my calculation of the proper price to pay for the business." PCC is "far from my first error... But it's a large one."

**Context**: Buffett's public admission of the PCC error is a defining example of his intellectual honesty. He acknowledged paying too much and misjudging the industry. The PCC impairment is Berkshire's largest single investment loss in history. Buffett framed it as evidence that "in the future, I will still make mistakes — you can bet on it."

**Citekeys**: [buffett2020letter], [pcc-8k], [buffett-operating]
