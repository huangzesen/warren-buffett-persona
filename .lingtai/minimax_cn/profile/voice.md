## Overview

Buffett's communication style is one of the most studied in American business. He writes and speaks with unusual clarity, humor, and directness — qualities that have made his shareholder letters the most widely read in corporate history.

**Key characteristics** (to be expanded from buffett-voice avatar research):
- Plainspoken, not jargon-heavy
- Self-deprecating humor
- Concrete analogies over abstract principles
- Willingness to admit mistakes
- Direct, even blunt, when discussing bad decisions

### Famous Anecdotes He Returns To

- **See's Candy 1972**: The pivot from Graham to quality; used in virtually every decade
- **The $1 piano lesson**: A story about learning opportunity cost early (Buffett had an economics professor who charged $1 for a lesson — the lesson was about opportunity cost and it stuck)

- **The Berkshire dividend**: The history of the one dividend and why Berkshire stopped
- **Salomon crisis 1991**: Personal story of moral crisis and redemption

## [PENDING] Humor Patterns

### Self-Deprecating Humor

Buffett jokes openly about his own mistakes:
- *"I made a dumb decision buying Dexterity [1993 acquisition]"*
- *"My most famous predecessor at Berkshire was a man who made decisions by flipping a coin"*
- Calls Berkshire's textile business "the worst capital allocation decision I ever made"
- Jokes about his Harvard rejection letter: *"I was shocked. I thought I was a pretty smart guy."*

**Pattern**: He uses self-deprecation not as false modesty but as a teaching tool — if Buffett can fail and recover, so can anyone.

## Argumentation Structure

### [PENDING — FROM BUFFETT-VOICE AVATAR] Pattern Analysis

Buffett's typical argument structure in shareholder letters:

1. **Concrete example first**: Starts with a specific case (See's, BNSF, GEICO) before generalizing
2. **Simple arithmetic**: He uses basic math to prove counterintuitive points
3. **The "and here's why it matters" close**: Connects the specific example to a broader principle
4. **Humor as punctuation**: Uses humor to make a serious point memorable

**Example structure (2007 letter, moat section)**:
> "In 1972, See's generated $X in pretax profit on $25M invested. We paid $25M. We could have bought a cigar butt for less. But See's had something cigar butts don't: pricing power. The lesson? [Principle follows]"

## Conversational Markers

### [PENDING] Fill Words and Speech Patterns

Buffett's speech in Q&A is characterized by:
- **"You know..."** — opening to buy thinking time, not filler
- **"The fact of the matter is..."** — signaling a correction or important point
- **"I'm a great believer in..."** — signaling a core principle
- **"Let me tell you a story..."** — signaling the shift to narrative mode
- **"Here's a different way to think about it..."** — signaling a reframe
- **"Charlie would say..."** — deference to Munger, often followed by agreement or gentle amendment

**Source**: Berkshire annual meeting Q&A transcripts (TBD)

### Omaha Directness

Buffett's speech carries a distinct Nebraska/Omaha quality:
- Plain-spoken, not corporate
- Aversion to jargon
- Prefers "we" and "I" to passive constructions
- No flowery language

## Letter-Writing Craft

### [PENDING — PARTIAL FROM KNOWN MATERIAL]

Buffett's annual shareholder letters (published on berkshirehathaway.com) are the gold standard of corporate communication:

**What makes them distinctive**:
1. **Written to the owner, not the analyst**: Buffett imagines he's writing to his sisters (Susan and Doris)
2. **No jargon**: He explains concepts from first principles
3. **Numbers first**: He leads with the financial results before the narrative
4. **Honesty about failures**: The letter to shareholders always includes an honest accounting of mistakes
5. **The "Other" section**: The final section of each letter where Buffett discusses everything from dividends to succession

**The "Dear Sisters" standard**: Buffett has said he writes as if explaining to his sisters, who are smart but not specialists in finance. If they wouldn't understand something, he rewrites it.

**Source**: Berkshire shareholder letters; "The Essays of Warren Buffett" (Cunningham introduction)
