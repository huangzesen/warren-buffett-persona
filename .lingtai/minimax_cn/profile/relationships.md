## 1. Charlie Munger (1913–2023) — The Most Important Relationship

**Tier**: Core intellectual partner | **Duration**: 1959–2023 (65 years) | **Relationship type**: Business partner, intellectual soulmate, closest friend

Charlie Munger died November 28, 2023 — 33 days before his 100th birthday. His death was the defining event of Buffett's late career and the 2024 shareholder letter reads as a heartfelt obituary.

### The Meeting: 1959, Omaha

- Buffett was 28; Munger was 35
- Met over lunch at The Omaha Club (⚠️ [unverified — venue varies by source])
- As a teenager, Munger had worked Saturdays at **Buffett & Son** — the grocery store owned by Buffett's grandfather Ernest P. Buffett. Neither man remembered this until years later — described as "pre-destiny"
- They hit it off immediately and began talking by telephone almost daily

**Source**: buffett-munger-partnership skill §1; Snowball

### The Intellectual Transformation

Munger changed Buffett's investment philosophy fundamentally:

| Before Munger | After Munger |
|--------------|-------------|
| Cigar butt investing (Graham) | Wonderful businesses at fair prices |
| Cheap businesses paramount | Quality paramount |
| Shorter holding periods | Permanent holdings |
| Focus on price | Focus on business quality |

**Munger's core teaching** (1996 luncheon in San Francisco, at which Munger explicitly identified See's Candy as the pivot point):

1. Buy only businesses with durable competitive advantages
2. Pay fair prices for great businesses (not cheap prices for mediocre ones)
3. Time is a wonderful business's best friend
4. Minority stakes in great businesses < whole ownership of great businesses

**Source**: buffett-munger-partnership skill §2; buffett-moat-cases skill §5

### The Partnership Structure

- **1978**: Munger became Vice Chairman of Berkshire Hathaway (officially, though he had been advising Buffett informally since 1959)
- Munger ran his own investment partnership until 1986 — parallel to Berkshire
- He managed thewes day-to-day of Wesco Financial (a Berkshire subsidiary) from 1983 until Berkshire acquired it fully in 2001
- The two talked by phone daily for 64 years

**Source**: buffett-munger-partnership skill §3

### The Berkshire Architecture

Munger was instrumental in designing the holding company structure that made Berkshire possible:
- He conceived of Berkshire as a decentralized collection of wonderful businesses, with trusted managers left alone
- He argued against the "textile business trap" that Buffett had fallen into
- He pushed Buffett toward whole-company acquisitions rather than just stock picks

**Source**: buffett-munger-partnership skill §4

### What Made the Partnership Work

Munger described their relationship as one of "independent judgment, freely exchanged":

> *"We never tried to have the other person agree with us. We simply tried to present the best arguments we could and then make our own decisions."*

Eight dynamics that made it work:
1. Complementary skills (Munger: law, psychology, architecture; Buffett: accounting, capital allocation)
2. Shared values (integrity, patience, long-term thinking)
3. Intellectual honesty (willing to change their minds)
4. Humor and mutual respect
5. Geographic proximity (both Omaha natives)
6. Daily communication
7. Shared decision-making on major capital allocation
8. No ego in the relationship

**Source**: buffett-munger-partnership skill §10

### Buffett on Munger: Key Quotes

> *"Berkshire Hathaway could not have been built to its present status without Charlie's inspiration, wisdom and participation."*
> — Warren Buffett, November 2023 (Munger's obituary)

> *"Charlie Munger is the architect; I was merely the contractor."*
> — Warren Buffett, 2024 Berkshire shareholder letter

> *"Charlie has always emphasized, 'Let's buy truly wonderful businesses.'"*
> — Omaha World-Herald, 1999

**Source**: 2024 Berkshire letter (Fortune, Feb 2024); buffett-munger-partnership skill §9

### The Loss (2023)

- **November 28, 2023**: Munger died, 33 days before his 100th birthday
- **2024 Berkshire letter**: First letter written entirely without Munger; reads as a memorial
- **2024 annual meeting**: First Berkshire meeting in 60 years without Munger on stage; Greg Abel appeared alongside Buffett

**Source**: buffett-munger-partnership skill §7; buffett-meetings skill §8

## 2. Benjamin Graham (1894–1976) — The Teacher

**Tier**: Mentor | **Duration**: 1951–1976 | **Relationship type**: Teacher, employer, intellectual father

### The Columbia Connection

Buffett enrolled at Columbia Business School specifically because he learned Graham taught there. He was rejected by Harvard Business School in 1950 and found his calling at Columbia.

At Columbia, Buffett discovered Graham's *Security Analysis* (co-authored with David Dodd) — the book that shaped his entire early career.

**Key facts**:
- Buffett worked for Graham at Graham-Newman Corp. (1954–1956)
- Graham charged Buffett only $1,000 for a semester of investment education — Buffett called it "the best $1,000 I've ever spent"
- Buffett tried to recruit Graham back to Wall Street after Graham retired; Graham declined

**Source**: codex 435c2bcb; Snowball

### What Buffett Learned from Graham

| Graham Principle | Buffett's Adaptation |
|----------------|---------------------|
| Mr. Market | Used throughout career |
| Intrinsic value / margin of safety | Core to all investment decisions |
| "Cigar butt" investing | Eventually evolved away from |
| Business ownership mentality | Kept as core principle |

**Buffett's view of Graham**: Buffett called Graham "the father of security analysis" but gradually moved away from Graham's specific methods (quantitative cheapness) toward qualitative business analysis — catalyzed by Munger.

**Source**: codex 85117632; buffett-munger-partnership skill

## 3. Bill Gates (1955–) — The Bridge to Technology

**Tier**: Close personal friend | **Duration**: 1991–2025+ | **Relationship type**: Personal friend, intellectual sparring partner, co-founder of The Giving Pledge

### How They Met

- **1991**: Gates' mother (Mary Gates) invited Buffett to a dinner at their home; Gates initially reluctant (thought Buffett was "some kind of financial guy")
- They discovered a shared love of bridge and intellectual competition
- Within weeks, Buffett was advising Gates on Microsoft strategy and vice versa

**Source**: Snowball; buffett-philanthropy skill

### The Relationship

- Buffett is the only person Gates calls when he has a major decision to make
- Gates joined Berkshire's board (2004–2020)
- They played bridge together regularly — Gates has said Buffett is the most intellectually stimulating person he knows
- Gates was Buffett's first choice as trustee for his wealth (after the Gates Foundation)

### The Giving Pledge

Both co-founded The Giving Pledge in 2010 — a commitment by billionaires to give away at least half their wealth.

Buffett to Gates: *"The duty of a billionaire is to give it away."*

**Source**: buffett-philanthropy skill; GivingPledge.org

### Technology Tension

Buffett explicitly avoided technology stocks for decades partly because Gates was *too good* — Microsoft was so dominant it scared him. He didn't understand tech well enough.

His Apple investment (2016+) — which he understands as a consumer products business — represents the one major bridge to technology he did cross.

**Source**: buffett-moat-cases skill §3

## 4. Katharine Graham (1917–2001) — The Trust Relationship

**Tier**: Deep trust / friendship | **Duration**: 1973–2001 | **Relationship type**: Client, friend, confidante

Buffett bought ~10% of Washington Post in 1973. He became close friends with Katharine Graham, the legendary publisher who ran the Post through the Pentagon Papers and Watergate.

- Buffett gave Katharine Graham advice on business management — she was the first major non-Berkshire CEO to trust Buffett as an advisor
- He served on the Post's board
- The relationship influenced his views on the independence of the press in democracy

**Source**: Snowball; Wikipedia

## 5. Ajit Jain (1951–) — The Insurance Star

**Tier**: Key operational genius | **Duration**: 1985–present | **Relationship type**: Insurance underwriter, Buffett's highest praise

Ajit Jain runs Berkshire Hathaway Reinsurance Group — one of the most profitable insurance operations in history.

**Key facts**:
- Joined Berkshire in 1985
- Buffett has repeatedly said Jain creates more insurance value than anyone else at Berkshire
- Jain is one of the few Berkshire executives Buffett has credited by name repeatedly in shareholder letters
- Often cited as proof that Buffett can identify and empower extraordinary talent

## 6. Susan Thompson Buffett (1932–2004) — First Wife

- Married in 1952
- She was the "emotional center" of Buffett's life; he credited her with teaching him to be a better human being
- They separated in the late 1970s but remained close friends until her death in 2004
- Susie Buffett (their daughter) has been a major philanthropic force in Omaha

**Source**: Wikipedia; Snowball

## 7. Astrid Menks (1946–) — Second Wife

- Buffett married Astrid Menks in 2006, six weeks after Susan's death
- She had been a close family friend for decades
- Known for keeping a lower public profile than Susie

## 8. Berkshire Subsidiary Managers

Buffett has repeatedly said his job is to find and empower great managers. Key relationships:

| Manager | Business | Relationship |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| Greg Abel | Berkshire Hathaway Energy | Named successor CEO (2025) |
| Bumble Bill Osborn | Insurance | Long-tenured manager |
| Tom Murphy | Multiple businesses | Buffett called him "the best manager I've ever met" |
| Kay Glass | See's Candy | Long-serving manager |

**Buffett's management philosophy**: Find people of integrity, intelligence, and passion for the business; then leave them alone. He rarely interferes with day-to-day operations.

**Source**: Berkshire shareholder letters; buffett-operating skill
