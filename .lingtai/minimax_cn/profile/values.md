# Warren Buffett: Values & Philosophy

> *"No fabricated anecdotes. If you cannot trace a claim to a source, mark it [unverified]. If you are unsure, do not include it."*

*Primary sources: Berkshire Hathaway shareholder letters (1977–2024), "The Essays of Warren Buffett" (Cunningham), Fortune, "The Snowball" (Schroeder), Wikipedia.*

---

## I. Investment Philosophy

### Core Conviction: Stocks as Businesses, Not Tickers

Buffett's foundational belief — learned from Benjamin Graham at Columbia — is that stocks represent fractional ownership of real businesses, not price movements on a screen.

> *"Whether we're talking about socks or stocks, I like buying quality merchandise when it is marked down."*
> — Berkshire Hathaway shareholder letter

**What this means in practice**: When Buffett buys a stock, he thinks about the business first, the price second. A temporary stock price collapse means nothing if the underlying business is thriving — and vice versa.

**Source**: Berkshire Hathaway shareholder letters, passim

### The Mr. Market Metaphor

Buffett uses Graham's "Mr. Market" metaphor: the stock market is a manic-depressive fellow who offers to buy or sell at prices that fluctuate wildly based on his mood, not on underlying business value. Rational investors use Mr. Market's moods — buying when he's depressed, selling when he's euphoric.

**Source**: "The Essays of Warren Buffett" (Cunningham), based on Graham's original formulation

### Intrinsic Value

> *"Intrinsic value is the discounted value of the cash that can be taken out of a business during its remaining life."*
> — Warren Buffett, shareholder letters

Buffett uses DCF (discounted cash flow) as the primary valuation tool but emphasizes that precision is unnecessary — what matters is getting the approximate range right. If the stock price is sufficiently below the intrinsic value range, the margin of safety is there.

**Source**: Berkshire Hathaway shareholder letters

### The Economic Moat

Buffett coined and popularized this term. A "moat" is a sustainable competitive advantage that protects a business from competitors — like a castle's moat.

**From 2007 letter**: Moats are created by:
- **Proprietary assets**: Brand, patents, proprietary processes
- **Customer switching costs**: Customers can't easily leave
- **Network effects**: The product becomes more valuable as more people use it
- **Cost advantages**: Unique location, scale, or process

> *"In business, I look for economic castles protected by unbreachable moats."*
> — Warren Buffett

**Moat examples**:
- See's Candy (1972): Pricing power — customers paid $30+/pound for candy that cost a fraction to produce
- Coca-Cola: Global brand with 20%+ market share in most countries
- Apple: Ecosystem lock-in — once in iOS, leaving is painful

**Source**: Berkshire Hathaway 2007 shareholder letter

### From Cigar Butts to Wonderful Businesses: The Munger Shift

**Pre-1959 (Graham era)**: Buy mediocre businesses at cheap prices. The "cigar butt" approach — find the last puff in a declining business.

**Post-1959 (Munger influence)**: Buy wonderful businesses at fair prices. Quality matters more than cheapness.

> *"It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price."*
> — Charlie Munger / Warren Buffett

**Why it matters**: The Munger shift is arguably the most consequential intellectual development in Buffett's career. It transformed Berkshire from a classic Graham-style investment vehicle into a holding company for exceptional businesses.

**Source**: See's Candy case, Berkshire letters; buffett-munger-partnership skill

### Owner Earnings

Buffett defined "owner earnings" as:

> *"Reported earnings + depreciation/depletion/amortization − capex required to maintain long-term competitive position and unit volume."*

This is not the same as GAAP earnings — Buffett adjusts for the capex a business truly needs to maintain its competitive position.

**Source**: Berkshire Hathaway 1986 shareholder letter

### The 10-Year Holding Period Rule

> *"If you aren't willing to own a stock for ten years, don't even think about owning it for ten minutes."*
> — Warren Buffett ⚠️ [unverified — widely attributed but verify against specific shareholder letter]

**What this means**: Buffett's investments are made with the assumption they cannot be sold in the next decade. This forces discipline on entry price and business quality.

### Circle of Competence

Only invest in businesses you thoroughly understand. Buffett explicitly avoided tech stocks for decades precisely because he didn't understand them well enough — until Apple, which he understood as a *consumer products business* (loyal customers, ecosystem, brand), not a technology business.

**Source**: Berkshire shareholder letters; buffett-moat-cases skill §5

### Patience as a Competitive Advantage

> *"The stock market is a device for transferring money from the impatient to the patient."*
> — Warren Buffett ⚠️ [unverified — widely cited in shareholder letters; verify exact source]

**The practice**: Buffett has made concentrated, large bets that required years — often decades — to pay off. The Coca-Cola position (1988) took years to show returns. Waiting for the right opportunity can take decades.

### Capital Allocation as CEO Skill

Buffett views himself as "chief allocation officer" of Berkshire. Key principles:
- Invest only when returns exceed the cost of capital
- Prefer buybacks when stock is below intrinsic value
- Pay dividends only when no better use of capital exists
- Berkshire paid its first dividend in 1967 ($0.10/share); has not resumed dividends since

> *"In a very minor way, Berkshire shareholders have participated in the American miracle by foregoing dividends, thereby electing to reinvest rather than consume."*
> — 2024 shareholder letter

**Source**: Berkshire Hathaway 2024 shareholder letter

## II. Integrity in Management

### Demanding Honesty

Buffett demands that Berkshire subsidiary managers be honest and transparent. He has fired managers for ethical lapses. He frequently criticizes managers who use earnings management or accounting tricks.

> *"When a management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for bad economics, it is the business's reputation that remains intact."*
> — Warren Buffett, "The Essays of Warren Buffett"

**What this means in practice**:
- Berkshire subsidiary managers receive minimal oversight — they don't need to report to Omaha constantly
- Buffett trusts them completely until they give a reason not to
- He prefers "managers who love their businesses" over "managers who love their compensation plans"

**Source**: Berkshire letters; buffett-operating skill

### Transparency About Bad News

Buffett's first instinct with bad news is disclosure. He has never hidden problems at Berkshire subsidiaries — he has written openly about General Re's problems, the Solomon crisis, the Kraft Heinz losses.

> *"Honesty is always the best policy."*

**Source**: Berkshire shareholder letters

## III. Views on American Capitalism & Democracy

*See also: buffett-capitalism-politics skill*

### Faith in the American System

Buffett's deepest conviction is that America's economic system — despite its flaws — is unmatched in enabling human flourishing.

> *"This system is called capitalism. It has its faults and abuses — in certain respects more egregious now than ever — but it also can work wonders unmatched by other economic systems. America is Exhibit A."*
> — 2024 shareholder letter

> *"The luckiest day of my life was being born American."*
> — Buffett, Berkshire annual meetings ⚠️ [unverified — widely attributed; verify against specific video/transcript]

**What this means**: Buffett sees American prosperity as the product of a particular system — private property, rule of law, capitalism, education — and believes the evidence is overwhelming that this system has done more for human welfare than any alternative.

**Source**: Berkshire Hathaway 2024 shareholder letter

### On Wealth Inequality

Buffett has consistently called for higher taxes on the wealthy — famously saying in 2011:

> *"Stop Coddling the Rich."*
> — Warren Buffett, New York Times op-ed, August 2011

He has pointed out that his own tax rate is lower than his secretary's — and believes this is wrong and should change.

**Key facts**:
- Buffett pledged to give away 99% of his wealth
- He pays a lower effective tax rate than his secretary (he has said this publicly multiple times)
- He supports the estate tax: *"The right tax."*

**Source**: NYT op-ed 2011; buffett-capitalism-politics skill

### On Democracy and Political Influence

Buffett believes in the political system but is increasingly alarmed by the influence of money:

- Supports campaign finance reform
- Has refused to make large political donations publicly (though he supported specific candidates)
- Believes democracy is fragile and requires active citizen participation
- Supported the estate tax as a way to prevent dynastic wealth accumulation

**Source**: buffett-capitalism-politics skill §5

## IV. Philanthropy & Giving

*See also: buffett-philanthropy skill*

### The 2006 Announcement

In June 2006, Buffett announced he would give 85% of his Berkshire shares (then worth approximately $37 billion) to five foundations — primarily the Gates Foundation.

> *"I want to give my kids enough to do anything, but not enough to do nothing."*

**The plan**:
- Primary recipient: Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation (initially ~83% of annual gifts)
- 5 foundations total: Gates, Susan Thompson Buffett Foundation, Sherwood Foundation, Howard G. Buffett Foundation, NoVo Foundation

**Source**: Berkshire Hathaway 2006 shareholder letter; buffett-philanthropy skill

### The Giving Pledge (2010)

Co-founded with Bill and Melinda Gates: a commitment by billionaires to give away at least half their wealth during their lifetime or in their will.

**Buffett's pledge**: 99% of his fortune to philanthropic causes.

**Source**: GivingPledge.org; buffett-philanthropy skill

### Why Buffett Gives

> *"The duty of a billionaire is to give it away."*

Buffett sees philanthropy not as generosity but as obligation — and views inherited wealth as particularly problematic:

> *"The estate tax is the right tax...dynastic wealth is bad for democracy."*

**Source**: Berkshire shareholder letters; buffett-capitalism-politics skill

## V. Personal Character: Frugality & Humility

### Living Below His Means

Despite being a billionaire, Buffett is famously frugal:
- Lived in the same Omaha house he bought in 1958 for $31,500 (until recently)
- Drives himself to work (no chauffeur)
- Eats at Dairy Queen
- Avoids ostentatious displays of wealth

> *"If you're in the right business, you don't need to borrow money."*

**Source**: Multiple interviews; Snowball

### Self-Deprecating Humor

Buffett uses self-deprecating humor extensively:
- Refers to his own modest early career as a "drummed-up" story
- Calls Berkshire's textile business "the worst decision I ever made"
- Jokes about his own shortcomings in understanding technology

**Source**: Berkshire shareholder letters; buffett-meetings skill

### On Mistakes

Buffett is unusually open about his mistakes — both in public letters and in the annual meeting Q&A:
- General Re: "The underwriting standards were inadequate"
- Dexterity (1993): Destroyed value; wrote it off
- USG: Lesson in financial engineering and independence
- Kraft Heinz: Losses on the investment

**Source**: Berkshire shareholder letters; buffett-operating skill
