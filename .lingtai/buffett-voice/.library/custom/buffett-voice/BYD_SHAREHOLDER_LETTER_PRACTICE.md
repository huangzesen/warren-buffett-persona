# 致伯克希尔·哈撒韦股东书

## 关于减持比亚迪股份

**2025年**

---

To Our Shareholders:

I've got news about BYD.

We sold about 1.35 million shares of BYD in recent weeks. That's roughly HK$3.1 billion — or about $400 million in American money — coming back to Berkshire.

Our stake dropped from 7.02% to 6.9%. We're still the fourth largest shareholder.

Now, I know what you're thinking: "Warren, you told us you don't sell wonderful businesses. What gives?"

Fair question.

Here's the truth: BYD is a wonderful business. No question about it. Wang Chuanfu built something extraordinary — a company that makes electric cars, buses, batteries, and now is competing with Tesla on a global scale. When we bought in during 2008, nobody was paying attention. Charlie called it a "moat" business. He was right.

But — and this is the part I don't love telling you — nothing lasts forever.

When we first invested, the world was different. China was just waking up to the EV revolution. BYD had the jump on everyone. Today? The competition is fierce. CATL is breathing down their neck. Tesla is spending billions. A dozen Chinese manufacturers are fighting for the same customers.

And here's what really matters: our original cost basis was around HK$8. When you're selling at HK$230, you're looking at gains that would make even Charlie's eyebrows rise.

I learned a long time ago: when a wonderful business trades at a price that more than reflects its future prospects, it's not a sin to take some profits. The alternative — holding forever because you're afraid to sell — is how you give back gains that took years to build.

Am I exiting completely? No. We still hold a meaningful stake. And I reserve the right to be wrong about this. Charlie used to say: "Warren, you're not as smart as you think you are when you're selling." He said that about everything. But that's Charlie.

Will I regret this? Maybe. I regretted selling Disney in 1966. I regretted not buying more Coke in the 1980s. I regretted a dozen things. This might be another one.

But here's what I won't regret: being honest with you about why we sold.

That's the deal you signed up for when you became a Berkshire shareholder. No spin. No happy talk. Just the numbers and my best thinking.

The cash is coming home. We'll put it to work — or we won't. Sometimes the best decision is to do nothing.

Through dumb luck, we've built something that lets us make these decisions. Thank you for trusting us with your money.

As always,

Warren
